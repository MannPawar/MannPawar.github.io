Agent-Based Modeling

Generative Opinion Dynamics in Skyrim

Emergent political belief & generative NPC dialogue
A stochastic agent-based model that evolves the political opinions of 1,010 named Skyrim NPCs through the Civil War: bounded-confidence influence, out-group repulsion, zealot anchoring, and a hold-clustered social network. To prove the social machinery (not the events) drives the outcome, it is tested against an energy-matched null with identical shocks: the structured model produces sharp echo chambers the null cannot. The emergent opinion of each character then conditions a local language model that voices them.
Beats an energy-matched null: separation 0.41 vs 0.24 · neutral-zone <1% vs 21% · KS D = 0.37 · 1,010 NPCs
Note: I re-ran the model with the social rules replaced by random motion of the same size. The realism disappeared: NPCs no longer took clear sides and the two campaigns barely diverged. So the believable structure is produced by the model, not by chance or the battles.

Optimization · Live demo

Stochastic Valorant Shotcaller

MILP + ML + MDP team-economy optimizer
A data-driven economy optimizer for Valorant: a Mixed-Integer Linear Program (SciPy HiGHS) assigns team-wide loadouts, an XGBoost model trained on 2.9M round samples predicts round-win probability, and a Markov Decision Process solved by backward induction factors long-term economy value. An LLM layer adds context-aware tactical constraints. In a paired simulation the EV-aware solver beats a naive save/buy rule by a clear, statistically significant margin. Deployed as a live interactive app.
XGBoost round-win model (AUC 0.71) · EV-aware solver +0.87 rounds/match vs naive (+7.2%, 95% CI 0.79 to 0.95, p < 1e-89) · live app
Note: a model learns each round's win odds, and the optimizer buys to maximize them while protecting the future bank. Across 1,000 simulated matches it wins almost one extra round per match versus a simple "save or full-buy" rule. The comparison is paired (same matches for both), so the edge is reliable, not luck.

Optimization

Skyrim Class Optimization

A mechanistic model of why players pick the classes they do
A first-principles model of Skyrim class choice built to explain behavior, not just forecast it. It weighs three forces on the probability simplex, combat efficiency, expressive build-complexity, and diversity-seeking, solved with LP/MILP (SciPy HiGHS, Gurobi). The finding: no single force works. Pure efficiency-maximizing collapses to 100% Stealth, which players never do; all three together cut error 48% below uniform. Because it is mechanistic, it can run counterfactuals a statistical prior cannot, such as "rebalance a class and predict how the population shifts."
48% lower error than uniform · isolates the 3 forces behind class choice · a mechanistic / counterfactual tool
Note: this explains why players choose as they do. Pure power-gaming would make everyone a Stealth assassin, but players don't, they also value variety and complex, expressive builds. Modeling all three forces together explains the real mix far better than any one alone, and unlike a model that just memorizes today's numbers, it can answer "what if we rebalanced a class?"

ML & Analytics

The Silent Killers of Player Retention

Exploratory churn analysis across live-service titles
A data pipeline over Steam's top titles: review text anchored to playtime telemetry to estimate a playtime-weighted "churn point" (when veterans drop off), with a local DeepSeek-R1 model surfacing the recurring themes behind it. Directional finding: design and progression issues, not monetization, dominate the qualitative complaints of long-time players. The themes are LLM-labeled and the metric is a proxy, so this is framed as exploratory market intelligence rather than a validated causal claim.
47 titles · playtime-weighted churn proxy · LLM-surfaced themes (exploratory)
Note: across 47 live-service games I estimated how many hours in players tend to drop off (weighting reviews by playtime), then had a local AI read the reviews and group the reasons. It points to design and progression problems over monetization, but it is an exploratory read of player opinions, not a measured cause.

About

I completed my MS in Business Analytics at William & Mary in May 2026, where I focused on turning messy behavioral data into models that can explain, simulate, and optimize complex systems. My independent work applies that toolkit to games: agent-based simulation, mathematical optimization, and machine learning for worlds where characters, players, and systems adapt to one another.

I am most interested in game AI that produces behavior rather than scripts it. The projects here model how NPC populations form beliefs, how teams make strategic decisions under uncertainty, and how player behavior changes over time, with each result checked against clear baselines and packaged so the work can be reproduced.

I am now applying to PhD programs in game AI, with a focus on believable agents, emergent social systems, and generative tools for interactive worlds. All code and documentation for the projects above lives on my GitHub.
